Key Milestones in the Development of Computers

The 1946 Eniac

The Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer was developed by
John Presper Eckert and John W. Mauchly at the University of Pennsylvania.
According to CNBC.
Based on information from Computer History at the time of its creation,
the U.S. Army needed to know the calculations of
ballistics tables. Eniac was the solution. It was the first to run at electronic speed without the use
of mechanical parts. Up until 1955, Eniac probably completed more calculations
than mankind had even done up to that point in history. Britannica says it used
plugboards for communication. The advantage to this was that once the instructuions
were computed, the Eniac completed them at electronic speed. The disadvantage
of the Eniac was that it would take days to rewrite the instructions for it to complete the calculations.
Despite its disadvantages, it was still the strongest calculating device ever created at that point in history.
Not only did it turn the tide for computers, it turned the tide for our country in World War 2.

IBM's Personal Computer 1981

IBM released the personal computer in August of 1981. Others had been
available before it but IBM's laid a foundation for future PC's. It costed
$1,565 and had 40K of read only memory and 16K of user memory. It also came
with a built in speaker and it could run self-diagnostic checks. This information
came from CNBC. According to IBM it was created by Bill Lowe. When he
first introduced the plan to build one, many of the executives were skeptical about it.
Nevertheless, they told him to come back with a proposal in 2 weeks. During those 2
weeks Lowe picked out a group of 12 strategists and together they worked very long
hours to come up with an effective proposal. They had to develop plans for the hardware,
software, manufacturing setup and sales strategy. When the PC was made, it did not just
process information but, it could conect to the home tv set, play games, and process text.

Apple’s Macintosh 1984

Steve Jobs released the first Macintosh in 1984. It had the ability to
talk and at the time had an advanced drawing application as well.
The Macintosh was designed for 2 main markets, the knowledge workers
and the college workers. I found this at CNBC. According to History Computer
it was the first commercially successful PC to feature the mouse and graphical
user interface. What's special about Apple's Macintosh is that they created all their
hardware and their own operating system unlike IBM PC's. The development of the
Macintosh began in the 1970s wit Jef Raskin. Jef Raskin was an apple employee who
wanted an easy,low-cost computer for the average person. He wanted to name the computer
after his favorite kind of apple, the Mcintosh. However, the name had to be changed due to
legal reasons. "The very first Macintosh board, which was designed by Burrell Smith, had 64
kilobytes (KB) of RAM, used the Motorola 6809E microprocessor, and was capable of supporting
a 256×256 pixel black-and-white bitmap display."-History Computer

Microsoft Launches Windows 1985

Windows 1.o was releaseed in November 1985 as an operating system with a
graphical interface, and millions of people still use Windows today. I got this from CNBC.
According to History Computer, on November 10 1983, Microsoft Corporation met at Plaza
Hotel in New York City. They presented a graphical user interface for MS-DOS which was
designed for IBM PC and compatible computers since 1981. It was first deveolped under the name
Interface Manager, but before its debut in 1985, the marketing staff persuaded Bill Gates to name it
Windows instead. IBM and Mircosoft had been official partners since 1981 because the MS-DOS
operating system became really successful with IBM computer. Microsoft Windows 1 was first sold
for $100 in 1985 with its main competition being Apple's Macintosh. Windows 1 actually got bad reviews
with people saying it was buggy, crude, and slow. This tough beginning was made even tougher when
Microsoft was threatened with a lawsuit from Apple. Tensions with Apple would continue to rise but
eventually Microsoft would gain steady ground with the release of Windows 2.

The World Wide Web 1989

In 1989, a British computer scientist by the name of Tim Berners-Lee had
thought of a “distributed information system” at CERN, which is the European
Organization for Nuclear Research, located in Geneva, Switzerland. One year
after, the fist website and server went live at CERN. I got this information from CNBC. According to
World Wide Web Foundation his idea really was not that popular at first and his boss had concerns.
The web was never really an actual CERN project. He began to work on it using an NeXT computer
which was one of Steve Jobs' earlier products. In October of 1990, Tim had written 3 fundamental
things that are at the base of today's web: HTML, URI, and HTTP. When the web began to grow,
he realized it wouldnt reach its maximum potential unless anyone could use it without having to pay
or ask permission. After some time, Tim and others at CERN agreed to make the code free for forever.
This decision was announced in April, 1993.
